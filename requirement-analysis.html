Requirement Analysis

  • Skill Classification: Technical Soft and Hard


  • Related Software Engineering Area(s) : Software Requirement

Requirement analysis is the process of identifying, validating and documenting specifications for the software engineering project. The main activities of requirement analysis are to detect and resolve the conflicts of the requirements, discover the bounds of the software and how it must interact with its organizational and operational environment, elaborate system requirements to derive software requirements. Requirements analysis taking care of describing the requirements precisely enough to enable the requirements to be validated, their implementation to be verified, and their costs to be estimated.

Rationale

Importance

Software requirement is one of the most important disciplines in software engineering. Studies shown that it costs 5 times more to detect and fix requirements defects during design, 10 times more during implementation and 20 times more during testing and 200 times more after system delivery. All of these emphasizing the fact that the more we put energy on analysis and validation of the requirements the more it will have long terms benefits for us not only during the development phase but also during deployment and maintenance.

Inclusion

Software Requirement is an inevitable part of any software development. Regardless of the type, domain and scale of the software requirement is an initial phase of development. These requirements which elicited in the requirement elicitation phase mostly are incomplete and inconsistent. The requirement analysis resolves these inconsistencies and make the requirement document compatible with each other.

Roles

Related Roles

Software Requirement Elicitator

Software Requirement Analyst and Software Requirement Elicitator work so closely, even these two role can be held by one person. The Software Requirement Elicitator will elicit the requirements that the Software Requirement Analysis will work on.

Software Requirement Analyst

Software Requirements Analysts form a bridge between clients and software developers. Their job is to fully understand the needs of the client or stakeholder who is ordering software. They must ensure they know exactly how the client needs the software to function and what kind of tasks they must be able to perform.

Stakeholder

Stakeholders are people or organizations who will be affected by the system and who have a direct or indirect influence on the system requirements.In another terms, stakeholder is anyone whose jobs will be altered, who supplies or gains information from it, or whose power or influence within the organization will increase or decrease.

Activities and Artifacts

Requirements Classification

Requirements can be classified on a number of dimensions. For example: Whether the requirement is functional or nonfunctional, Whether the requirement is derived from one or more high-level requirements or an emergent property, Whether the requirement is on the product or the process. The requirement priority. The higher the priority, the more essential the requirement is for meeting the overall goals of the software.

Identification of overlapping statements

Specifications overlap if one or more of their components designate common aspects of the system under development, its domain and environment, and the process of construction. The existence of an overlap may imply consistency rules between specifications, which if breached make them inconsistent. A breach of a consistency rule generally manifests itself as a logical inconsistency, that is the assertion of a fact and its negation for a pair of overlapping components. Overlap and inconsistency are two different levels of interference between specifications, the first of which is prerequisite for the second. Based on the texture of the conflict we may use different techniques including: Informal detection, Formal detection (logic based), Heuristic detection, Lightweight detection.

Identification and documentation of inconsistencies and conflicts

Requirements can be inconsistent with each other due to theior origin because multiple stakeholders with different focous and concern may produce different reuirements in requirement elicitaton phase. Conflicts are important type of inconsistency, which deserves special attention.
A standard documentation technique consists of building an interaction matrix.

Inconsistency Handeling and conflict resolution

This concerns resolving problems with requirements where conflicts occur between two stakeholders requiring mutually incompatible features, between requirements and resources, or between functional and nonfunctional requirements.

Conceptual Modeling

The development of models of a real-world problem is key to software requirements analysis. Their purpose is to aid in understanding the situation in which the problem occurs, as well as depicting a solution. Hence, conceptual models comprise models of entities from the problem domain, configured to reflect their real-world relationships and dependencies. This topic is closely related to the Software Engineering Models and Methods KA.

Role of Academia/Industry

Requirement analysis is a challenging skill as it contains both hard skills (conflict identification, resolution, formal and informal analysis…) and soft skills (negotiation, brainstorming…) from the other side it is prone to risk because there are certain amount of uncertainty regarding each of the requirements as well as advert consequences in case requirement did not analyzed and documented in the right way. Both academia and industry can have a significant role in cultivating this issue each of which in different way:

Role of Academia


1- In academia mostly theory courses are following with a project in which students practice the theory content practically. In academia stakeholders are professor and the other teammates. Professor plays the role of the stakeholder and the customer at the same time and (s)he is always there to respond to students questions and he is at the same place with students.

2- In academia the requirements are mostly stable they will not change so there is no need to go back and forth in the requirement specifications process for multiple times.

3- In academia, the requirements are conveyed by specialized people so this is very unlikely that we have error in the requirements in its origin.

Role of Industry


1- Focus is on the project itself not the learning so there is no prior education for requirement analysis and the analyst should know in advance some concepts like formal and informal analysis or UML diagraming.

2- In industry our clients are not necessarily educated I software engineering so the requirements they give us is not in the form of requirements even they are a bunch of demand and this is the requirements analyst to give the meaning to each requirement.

3- In industry the stakeholders may not be in the same place as the requirement analyst. This makes the communication hard also it is possible that they cannot be responsive at all the times.
